How Did Susan Kare Become the Mother of Modern UI?
_

A World Without Susan Kare

Circa 1982, many consumers would have become accustomed to the steep and discouraging learning curve of having a computer. At the time, Graphical User Interfaces didn't exist, as the majority of all commercial and personal computers would have used CLI or Command Line Interface, which many nowadays would contribute to the aesthetics of spy movies and hackers, as users would have to learn several dozen lines of code to complete seemingly simple computational tasks. There was no need for a mouse, as only typing was required, it was highly specific and any errors would halt your progress drastically. However, when personal and home computers became more mainstream, providers would have to modify the setup of the computer to make it more user-friendly and to increase ease of access for the public.
This is when UI was born.

The MacIntosh personal computer marked the new year, released on January 24th, setting a precedent for all technology that would follow. It was the first commercially successful personal computer, boasting both a mouse and graphical user interface, which had been employed by the previous computer, the Xerox PARC Alto personal computer, which failed in the market despite the newly invented bitmap graphics. However, the success wouldn't have been granted if not for Susan Kare, who had only been hired the year prior with no experience regarding computer graphics or typefaces. However, she did have a degree in fine arts and graphic design, and with a growing interest in technology, she couldn't refuse the offer.

One of the biggest challenges in designing the icons was the limitation of the screen resolution at the time. The early Macintosh had a low screen resolution of 72 dpi or dots per inch, so each icon had to be very simple, clean, and recognizable at small sizes around 32x32 pixels or 64x64 pixels. Her background in art helped considerably with this as she compared the work to that of mosaic or needlepoint, working on small precise scales, bit by bit. Kare used visual metaphors to create these icons. For example, the trash can icon was designed to represent a place where discarded files went just like a real trash can. This immediately conveyed the idea to users without needing any explanation. Some symbols however were more thought out than others, such as the “cut” and “boot” icons, and the command icon “⌘” was inspired by the Sweedish symbol for “a point of interest”, as she had seen the symbol on road signs while travelling. Even more interesting is that many sources say that the symbol means a place of power instead, which isn't far off, as Kare received mail from someone stating that the loops are meant to represent the turrets on a castle, thus denoting the “power” or “command” aspect of the symbol.



Kare wanted the UI to make the concept of owning a computer easy to digest for the average consumer, to make it less intimidating, which is where the Happy Mac comes into place. The Happy Mac icon was designed by Kare to invite new users in and make them feel more at ease by creating a cute design that took the edge off the intimidating premise of computing. The icon itself is reminiscent of the Bauhaus logo, showing the expansive knowledge of art history that Kare had accumulated to use in her design arsenal. This is clearly shown later down the line when the Happy Mac icon evolved into the app finder icon, which has the same split face the Bauhaus has combined with the classic smile and shape of many of Kare’s original concepts for design, such as her “danger” icon.

After Kares influential time serving at Apple, in which she created the world's first set of widely used UI icons, symbols and displays, making technology more widely accessible to the public, made such iconic designs that have lasted decades of use, and made computers “less cold and intimidating” with fun, quirky and whimsical designs that defy the stereotype of evil devices with ulterior motives such as H.A.L. Or SkyNet, welcoming users and creating comfort in a contemporarily unknown experience, she took a step back and starting developing her design firm.



She would continue doing this for the next thirty years, acting as a design director for Microsoft, Facebook, Motorola, Intel and General Magic and IBM. while working at Microsoft, Kare designed one of the first digital solitaire games which was a method for teaching new users how to use the mouse, a relatively new addition to computers at the time, with a game that was well-known and simple enough that people would be able to learn the controls of the mouse without even knowing they’re learning.



In addition, she made several icons for Windows that were remanded in use from Windows 3.0 up until Windows XP. While still staying under the Microsoft umbrella, she worked on a collaborative project with IMB, OS2, an alternative to DOS, in which she designed not just the icons for the system, but colour dithering, a method which prevents colour banding, which is commonly seen when images are compressed to a significantly smaller size, in which any colour gradients are segmented, becoming noticeable and ruin the image quality, while dithering uses a noise effect to create smoother gradients.

In 2015, Kare was hired by Pinterest, her first corporate and salaried job since she left Apple thirty years ago, and served as their lead product designer. She worked on the mobile and desktop “Brio” rebrand, where she created several icons and revamped many of the older ones, such as the loading cursor being a rotating button in reference to the arts and crafts and DIY culture on the site. Kare's portfolio of icons for this site is more rounded and bubbly, creating a more fun and cohesive style for the site. In an interview with John Brownlee from FastCompany, Kare compared the company with early Apple, saying that the creative flow and team were so similar that it was comforting, that she was so pleased to see that the user-first experience that Apple was providing back in the 1980s hadn’t died, stating that the slogan for the Pinterest team was to “put pinners first”.

The question of how Susan Kare became the mother of modern UI feels like it needs no answer. Kare has built a legacy with the most megalithic corporations creating their brand image, and the most recognisable parts of the brand that we as consumers would interact with almost daily, be it on our phones, tablets, laptops or otherwise, her influence is everywhere. The icons that she pioneered for the iOS system and Apple brand have stayed in effect until this day in some shape or form, with the designs either being adapted throughout the years or simply remaining as she created. She also sought not just to make accessible designs but to create welcoming ones, thinning the barrier between the public and computers, something that I believe is still relevant today, as technology becomes more advanced, many are expected to have a higher ad higher level of technical literacy, while many elderly and those unexposed to the digital world will more than likely struggle to adapt to the expectations of today's society, and in that regard, we should look back to Kares philosophy of making the digital world friendlier for all. Without Susan Kare's work, we would most likely be significantly behind technologically, not far out of the shadow of the command line interface and imputing all code manually to execute it, and that is the reason she is the mother of not just modern UI, but modern technology as a whole.
